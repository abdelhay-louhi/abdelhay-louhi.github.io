Manifesto

Data science is the
science of all sciences

Every science is, at its core, the same act repeated: observe the world, find the pattern, build the model, test it. Kepler did data science when he mapped planetary orbits — he was doing exactly what we do today with neural networks and inference engines. The spirit has never changed. Only the instruments evolved.

What makes data science singular is that it belongs to no domain. It is the universal method. A physicist uses it to probe dark matter. A biologist uses it to decode genetic expression. A cosmologist uses it to reconstruct the structure of the universe from noise. The data changes; the discipline of asking and answering does not.

Scientific understanding is the engine of everything humanity builds. Every technology that surrounds us is crystallised science. The transistor came from quantum mechanics. GPS came from general relativity. Medical imaging came from nuclear physics. The internet's architecture came from information theory. You cannot build well what you do not understand deeply.

This is why I care as much about understanding as about building. The scientist who grasps the underlying laws will always engineer more cleverly than the one who only applies the recipe. Cross-domain knowledge is the sharpest competitive advantage a problem-solver can have — and it is what I mean when I call myself a data scientist.

C++ · Evolutionary Algorithms · Combinatorics
Simulating a Rubik's Cube | Testing Genetic Algorithms to solve it (All form scratch)
Built a Rubik's Cube simulator in C++ from scratch, then let evolution find a solver — no group theory, no heuristics. Pure evolutionary search over 4.3 × 10¹⁹ states.
›

Built a complete Rubik's Cube in C++ from scratch, encoding the cube as a permutation group. Then fed it to thousands of virtual agents running a genetic algorithm: selection pressure, crossover over move sequences, mutation. No group theory encoded by hand — evolution had to discover it.

💡 The hardest part was not the simulation — it was designing a fitness landscape over 4.3 × 10¹⁹ states that would actually guide evolution somewhere useful. Framing the problem turned out harder than solving it.
